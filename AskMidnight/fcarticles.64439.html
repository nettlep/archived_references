C-Buffers And Z-Buffers
Question submitted by (03 August 2000)
 
  Hello. I was just wondering- what would be the advantage of rendering to a cBuffer and a zBuffer? Thanks.  
 

 
  Here's a quick run-down. These are the key points. Depending on your situation, there may be other minor advantages or disadvantages that apply.

Advantages of a z-buffer:
  • Easily implemented in hardware.
  • Easily implemented in software.
  • Works well on any scene.
    • Disadvantages of a z-buffer:
  • Highly memory intensive (specialized hardware can avoid this drawback.)
  • Not well suited for higher-level visibility/occlusion culling.
  • Highly resolution dependent.
  • Prone to accuracy problems.
    • Advantages of the c-buffer:
  • Works well on scenes with large polygons (in these cases, faster than z-buffer and uses less memory.)
  • Not resolution dependent in the X-direction.
  • Works very well with higher-level visibility/occlusion culling algorithms.
  • Less prone to accuracy problems than z-buffers.
    • Disadvantages of the c-buffer:
  • Fast complexity growth with lots of small polygons in complex scenes.
  • Not as easily adapted for hardware as z-buffers.
  • Much trickier to code than a z-buffer (especially if you allow interpenetration.)
  • Resolution dependent in the Y direction (same as z-buffer.)

    There must be a confusing in terminology here. The c-buffer does not increase in complexity with lots of small
    polygons. This is because the c-buffer doesn't keep Z information for every span so you can join
    adjacent spans when inserting new polygons. This means that the number of spans will always be low
    and can at maximum be WIDTH/2 (with WIDTH the horizontal dimension of the screen) for one
    scanline. And this maximum is very hard to reach with realistic scense. Also the c-buffer complexity
    starts going down again when the screen is being filled completely because more and more spans are
    joined.

    The s-buffer is different though. The s-buffer keeps z information so you cannot join adjacent
    spans if they belong to different polygons.

    I am much suckier in 3D than both Jorrit and Paul, but I will barf my 2c out anyway:

    I believe the advantages and disadvantages arise from how you use the zbuffer and cbuffer. I will take for exampple Quake. For the walls, etc. they use a cbuffer algorithm. This is appropriate because they use BSP (meaning among other things that the polygons will not penetrate each other, so you can use cbuffer). If they are sorted front-to-back then you can join occluding spans. However, for the actors running around they use zbuffer. This is mainly because in Quake, they can penetrate each other sometimes. When the walls are drawn, the zbuffer is written to (not read from or tested against, so it's faster.)

    The cbuffer is only good if you're guaranteed that:
    Polygons don't penetrate one another (so one is guaranteed to be in front for the whole span)

    Additionally you get a benefit if:
    Polygons are sorted front-to-back.

    But note, it depends what you use the cbuffer for. Mainly it's done for software. If you use hardware, just stick with zbuffer, and most cards will handle this for you much quicker than any of your own cbuffer code.

    Also note: The c-buffer and z-buffer belong in the rendering of a scene. Use some algorithm to get a PVS (potentially visible system) of polygons. There are many out there... BSP, Portal, Octree, Beamtree, KD-tree, etc. (even some "holy grails" from what I've heard, which get a PVS from anything in real-time). Once you gt your PVS you use a c-buffer, zbuffer, or whatnot to actually draw those polgons with correct overlap. What method is better often depends on which PVS determination you used. Also, there are hw/sw considerations. Note that the PVS dtermination is independent fo the rendering method, hardware, software, cspan, sspan or whatnot.

    Harmless wrote some good articles on this.

    Jorrit has a good point about c-buffers. They do allow for spans to be joined. My point was that c-buffers can increase in their complexity, whereas the complexity of z-buffers is constant. I'm not trying to sound defensive, I just don't want this point lost on the reader.

    Also, quake doesn't use c-buffers (or even s-buffers as many people thought). Quake (last time I checked) uses AETs ([A]ctive [E]dge [T]ables.)
